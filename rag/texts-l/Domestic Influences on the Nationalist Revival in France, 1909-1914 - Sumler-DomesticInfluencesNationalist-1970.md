# Domestic Influences on the Nationalist Revival in France, 1909-1914

David E. Sumler

Historians have assumed that prior to World War I increasing international tensions dominated the thoughts and actions of European politicians to such an extent that they viewed domestic politics as of secondary importance. In the case of France the election of the revanchist Raymond Poincaré to the presidency of the Republic in January 1913 and the passage of the Three Years Law the following July have been attributed primarily to a "nationalist revival" occasioned by Germany's bellicose foreign policy.<sup>1</sup> Little attention has been paid to the fact that domestic political battles continued from the Separation to the outbreak of the war, and they became increasingly heated until French society, on the eve of the war, was severely polarized. In this context the nationalist revival appears to have been less a reaction to German pressure than an integral part of a conservative resurgence, a product of the domestic struggle for power between the center-right and center-left.

From 1900 to 1914 France, in common with the other great powers, experienced a rapid growth of the political left. After the Dreyfus Affair the Radical party had become the dominant party in the National Assembly and, with the cooperation of the anticlerical moderates and of socialists, had enacted the Separation. In the legislative election of 1906 the electorate endorsed both the Radical party and the policy of separation. Radicals and Radical-Socialists, controlling 247 votes out of 597 in the Chamber of Deputies, dominated the new legislature. The centrist position of the Radicals made it impossible to form a

Under the premiership of Georges Clemenceau the Radical-dominated Chamber enacted a series of reforms that alienated the anticlerical moderates who had supported the Separation but would not support social measures. Above all, these moderates were thoroughly alarmed when Clemenceau's Minister of Finance, Joseph Caillaux, proposed the enactment of a graduated income tax. To prevent such reforms the moderates began to cooperate with traditional, clerical conservatives and to lay the parliamentary basis for a conservative resurgence.

Aristide Briand, Clemenceau's successor as premier, provided the focus of this incipient coalition. Traditionally, the French premier delivers a major address to the nation each fall, just prior to the opening of the legislative session. Often these speeches are given merely to serve tradition. This was not true of Briand's speech given at Perigueux on October 10, 1909. To the contrary, in this speech Briand called for a new era in French politics. The time had come, he said, to heal the wounds caused by the bitter struggle over the Separation. ". . . We are at an hour when the country feels a great, an irresistible need for union, concord and brotherhood . . . ; it is necessary to end the hatred and fratricidal struggles among [France's] children. . . . We wish to give to all, without distinction of party, . . . justice . . . and the freedom to express their opinion. . . ."<sup>2</sup> This was a repudiation of the record of the Radical party. Briand implied that, in their nine years of dominance, the Radicals had nurtured strife among Frenchmen, had governed not for the nation but for their narrow constituencies, and had denied their opponents freedom of speech and justice in the courts. He then went on to warn that, by their insistence on such reforms as a state monopoly of education and a graduated income tax, the Radicals were threatening to prolong the divisions within the country.

With the intention of ending Radical dominance of the Chamber of Deputies, Briand became the first premier to endorse an electoral reform based on proportional representation. Under *représentation proportionelle* (*RP* in the contemporary press) all parties would receive seats in the chamber in proportion to the percentage of the popular vote which they received on a single ballot.<sup>3</sup> Politicians of both left

By endorsing *RP* Briand had begun a parliamentary struggle which was to last for three years. One should not suppose that the debate was prolonged merely by the electoral fears of legislators. The motivating force of the campaign for *RP* was the desire to weaken the Radical party.<sup>4</sup> Writing after the Radical landslide of 1906 Jacques Mars revealed the conservatives' fears about the *scrutin d'arrondissement*:

> A third fact to note is that in certain departments of France the opposition is being reduced to the state of an irretrievable minority . . . in spite of the relative strength of the opposition forces. . . .

The Radicals were just as aware of the implications of proportional representation. At an antiproportionalist rally in December 1909 the president of the executive committee of the Radical party spoke bluntly about the blessings of the single-member constituency: "I know what the constituency system has given us; I know that at each election our party ranks have swelled; I know that the majority system with a limited suffrage—perhaps too limited—has produced a Radical and Radical-Socialist majority even in the Senate. . . ."<sup>6</sup>

Together *apaisement* and *RP* offered the moderate conservatives of the Alliance républicaine démocratique (ARD) and the Fédération républicaine (FR) a practical anti-Radical program. From 1911 to 1914 this program became the focal point of French politics. Each ministry "either revived the idea of *apaisement* and national unity or, conversely, manifested a belief in the desirability of party strife."<sup>7</sup>

Two years of political equivocation elapsed between Briand's first formulation of these ideas and the appointment of Raymond Poincaré as premier, which signaled the success of Briand's policies. Running on basically anti-Radical programs in the legislative election of April 1910, both the center-right and the Socialist party made gains at the Radicals' expense. Three brief ministries followed in eighteen months. Briand remained in power by courting the conservatives until February 1911.<sup>8</sup> After Briand's fall the Radicals formed a loose coalition with the Socialists to support the next two ministries—those of Ernest Monis and Joseph Caillaux. Neither of these men could weld his majority into a stable alliance. Radicals and Socialists cooperated in May 1911 when Monis attacked railroad owners for mistreatment of their employees and again when he asked for a complete state monopoly of education. The leftist coalition also united behind Caillaux's insistence upon a graduated income tax. The main issue of debate in 1911, however, was none of these; it was electoral reform based on proportional representation. On this the Socialists split with the Radicals and voted with the conservatives for the reform. As long as *RP* remained the central political issue, no true left-right division of the Chamber was possible. For a stable, coherent majority to be formed, either *RP* had to be eliminated as an issue or another more important issue had to arise which would unite the Socialists and Radicals in spite of *RP*.

Both of these conditions were fulfilled under the Poincaré ministry, in office from January 1912 to January 1913. During these twelve months Poincaré, a senator from Lorraine identified with the moderate ARD, passed an electoral reform bill and initiated a policy of *fierté nationale*. Caillaux, who preceded Poincaré, was a Radical and an ardent champion of the income tax. He presented his cabinet to the Chamber of Deputies on July 1, 1911. On the same day a German gunboat steamed into the Moroccan port of Agadir. Morocco had long been a source of friction between France and Germany, and, on this occasion, the Germans were protesting French military movements there. Although Agadir caused a flurry of alarm in French newspapers for several days, the incident was overshadowed while the Chamber remained in session by the debate over *RP*. There was no immediate patriotic response from the press as certain nationalists later claimed.<sup>10</sup> During the legislative recess, newspapers carried almost daily accounts of diplomatic developments, but only rarely were editorials devoted to Franco-German relations.<sup>11</sup> For the most part the press reflected official optimism. After each short-lived crisis—as in mid-September when Caillaux required conscripts eligible for discharge to remain in active service—the press would return to domestic politics.

During October and November the conservative press was greatly disturbed by Caillaux's plans for the fall legislative session. The ministry that Poincaré formed was a cabinet of luminaries, a cabinet of "national union" which took all its members from the center of the political spectrum. It included Briand, Alexandre Millerand, Théophile Delcassé, Léon Bourgeois, and Jean Dupuy. Historians have argued that this ministry represented an irresistible tide of nationalism loosed by the Agadir crisis. For instance, Eugen Weber has written: "As ever in times of foreign danger, national union had been sought and found round a moderate Republican."<sup>18</sup> Yet, no one has suggested that there was any organized movement to further Poincaré's chances to become premier either before or after Caillaux's fall. In fact, President Fallières had first asked Léon Bourgeois and then Delcassé to form a cabinet before turning to Poincaré. The overture to Bourgeois was ritual. Delcassé, however, the obvious choice to form an anti-German cabinet, was not able to gain support.<sup>19</sup> Poincaré was probably a popular compromise.<sup>20</sup> He was not pushed forward by a surge of public sentiment or by a chauvinistic clique in parliament.

To the contrary, it was Poincaré who hoped to create a nationalist revival among his countrymen. Several days after Agadir Poincaré gave a speech before the ARD in which he called for a "national renaissance." Since the language he used was typical of conservative orators, there is no reason to think the speech was written in response to Agadir. But Poincaré added a new urgency to the old slogans:

Judging from this speech one might expect, then, that Poincaré would use the occasion of the presentation of his ministry to the chamber to call for a nationalist revival. Without going so far he did call for national unity. He said in opening that he had felt it his duty "to group, in a national spirit, all the factions of the republican party" and that in doing this he only reflected the nation's mood.<sup>22</sup> Poincaré then promised to sponsor the ratifications of the Franco-German treaty. The maintenance of public order, the unbending repression of crimes and offences against persons and property, and the regular functioning of the great administrative services are the elementary duties of all government. By his campaign of *fierté nationale* Poincaré had already begun the countermovement which was to drive the socialists and Radicals into an alliance at the end of 1913. Militarism had intensified antimilitarism which in turn spurred militarists and nationalists to still greater efforts. Eugen Weber, in his study of the nationalist revival in prewar France, has noted that "if the nationalist revival was influential, its scope was very limited."<sup>45</sup> It is fair to say that it was limited to politicians, journalists, and intellectuals who supported the center-right and right—that is, the parliamentary groups of the Democratic Left, Republican Union, Progressistes, Action libérale, Independents, and Right. Outside of this political and intellectual elite, it is probable that only the regular readers of the conservative press participated in the "revival". The more conservative Radicals wavered between support of and opposition to Poincaré's policy of national pride. When in 1913 a united Radical party offered a clear alternative, all but a few Radicals were willing to vote for measures that Poincaré's supporters could label antipatriotic.

They had begun this campaign during the Agadir crisis and continued it more or less constantly until the autumn of 1913. The SFIO and the leaders of the labor movement took no part in the nationalist revival; rather they demonstrated against it in the streets.<sup>46</sup>

The polarization of French politics which began with these events was completed by the fall of 1913. Three parliamentary confrontations of left and right marked this polarization and testified to the growing strength of the moderate right: the dissolution of the Syndicat des instituteurs in September 1912, the election of Poincaré to the presidency of the Republic in January 1913, and the passage of the Three Years Law on military service in July 1913.

The Syndicat des instituteurs, holding its national congress at Chambery, had voted on August 17, 1912, to support the Sou du Soldat, a society for spreading antimilitarist propaganda among soldiers.<sup>47</sup> Conservatives, especially clericals who considered the free-thinking school teachers a particular threat to society, were scandalized. Actually the concrete antimilitarist measures which the teachers advocated were quite mild: "subordination of the military to the civilian power; prohibition of the use of troops against strikers; the greatest possible reduction of the time spent [by conscripts] in active service; suppression of military prisons."<sup>48</sup> To the conservatives the teachers' action was an affront to the nation at a time when all Frenchmen should have united in patriotic support of Poincaré during his Russian visit.<sup>49</sup> Acting after the premier's return the minister of public instruction declared that the teachers' union must dissolve forthwith. The conservative press was exultant.<sup>50</sup> They saw the question as one of internal order as much as one of national defense. In an editorial praising the government for the dissolution *Le Temps* avoided any mention of national security and made only passing reference to patriotism. The passage of the Three Years Law was the culmination of the conservative resurgence which had begun in 1910. It also completed the polarization of French politics. The conservative resurgence was heralded by the right as a *renaissance nationale*. Whereas most historians have attributed this nationalist revival to the shock of Agadir, the men involved saw it as developing out of domestic politics. Such was the view of the editor of *L'Intransigeant*, writing several days after the final passage of the military law.

The political organization which tried to capitalize on this conservative resurgence was the Alliance républicaine démocratique, now transformed into the Parti républicain démocratique. In its appeal to public opinion the PRD confounded nationalism and antisocialism. The cloak of *apaisement*, which had been designed to bring a conservative coalition to power and to postpone social reforms, was now dropped for a more direct aggressive approach. An editorial in *Le Bulletin ARD* displayed this new confidence. "The republicans will have to choose between two tendencies, between two flags. They will have to decide between the Radical-Socialist Party, which on many points co-operates with the Socialist Party, and the *Parti républicain démocratique*, which aims at rallying all reforming republicans, resolute partisans of social evolution but also anti-collectivists and antisocialists."<sup>73</sup>

French conservatives would not have felt confident enough to issue such a challenge in 1909. Since then, however, largely on the basis of Poincaré's nationalist campaign, conservatives of all shades had formed a united front. This unity was substantially, if not entirely, a product of domestic politics. Other trends between 1906 and 1914, such as the rapid growth and increased militancy of organized labor and the geometric progression of socialist voting strength, lend additional weight to an emphasis on domestic politics. It was this threat from the extreme left as well as their desire to displace the Radicals that led moderates like Poincaré to appeal for "national union" on behalf of the social status quo.
