# LAND AND POLITICS IN
# EDWARDIAN ENGLAND

In conception, at least, like Tawney's study of the gentry, this Tawney Memorial Lecture seeks to put the economic structure of landed society into the framework of English political life. Its subject is what might be called the Edwardian revolution, possibly the greatest of all English aristocratic crises. The lecture falls into two main parts. The first deals with the finances and state of mind of great Edwardian landowners, particularly the finances and state of mind of Herbrand, 11<sup>th</sup> duke of Bedford, who was not only among the greatest of great landowners but was also in a way landed society's bellwether. The second deals with the politics of confrontation between great landowners and the Liberal Government, a confrontation that began with Lloyd George's 1909 Budget and ended with the mustering of armies, mainly in Ireland but also in England. It is a story of what had long been unknown in English society: aristocratic resistance to the duly constituted government.

To say that Bedford was landed society's bellwether needs qualification.<sup>1</sup> He was not a conspicuous public figure; on succeeding to the family estates he made them his chief business, declining high political office. He had, however, firm and often passionate opinions on public affairs, especially concerning the Army and the taxation of landowners. What he said about the Army he said mainly in the House of Lords where he dwelt heavily on the Haldane reforms and England's unpreparedness for war, and their lordships were apt to flee his pronouncements. For in it he touched a chord of fear common to landowners in the late Victorian and Edwardian eras—a fear for the future of their wealth and political power.

Such fear was not new. Ever since the French Revolution the shadow of catastrophe had on occasion flitted through English aristocratic minds. About the 1880s the shadow deepened and something like the mood of a beleaguered noblesse began to take shape. For the first time, as if better to protect themselves, many aristocratic politicians sought refuge in a single political party, the Conservative or Tory or Unionist party, and put forward claims for the special authority of their own House as against the authority of the House of Commons. They did so the better to fend off what they believed were the stratagems of latter-day Robespierres like Joseph Chamberlain in whom they saw the specter of an antilandlord state. This was the mood in 1889 which prompted Lord Salisbury's Government to instruct its ambassador in Paris to absent himself from the centenary celebration of the French Revolution.

Such fears may be both overblown and yet not without grounds—as these in the event proved to be. From 1880 to 1914 aristocratic power was variously threatened: on its political side by the civil war in Ireland, humiliation in the Boer War, and crushing defeat in the 1906 General Election; and on its economic side by a fall in farm rents, the rise of progressive taxation, and the land policies of Lloyd George. For a ruling elite which had long had things its own way these challenges not surprisingly made for resentment and resistance. They also nurtured that "menacing sense of decadence"<sup>2</sup> which pervaded the Edwardian mind and gave full play to the catastrophic imagination. Even that most imperturbable of men, the Conservative leader and philosopher, Arthur Balfour, chose as theme for the first Henry Sidgwick lecture in 1908 the fall of great societies, entitling it simply "Decadence."

Bedford's book gave this mood striking expression—and no doubt served to deepen it. Entitled *A Great Agricultural Estate* and published in 1897 the book had behind it the combined authority of the House of Russell's longstanding reputation as promoters of efficient agriculture and of the author's own budding reputation as a practical agriculturist. Bedford dealt with two of his estates, Woburn and Thorney, both agricultural estates comprising "some of the best wheat land in England." If he died, he noted, the new Harcourt death duties would levy a tribute of £25,000 on bankrupt Thorney alone. Faced by such perils Bedford exclaimed that men who did their duty by their dependents—and Bedford's outlay on his estates was indeed generous—and lost money by doing so, would get rid of their white elephants, thus ending financial loss and "the odium and anxieties attaching to the ownership of land."<sup>4</sup>

Bedford's book drew national attention. The reviewers saw it for what it was: the great landowners' manifesto, a refutation of critics of the English estate system, a claim that the system "secured to those dwelling on the soil material and moral advantages greater than any that are promised under any alternative system, tried or untried."<sup>5</sup> In many quarters it was hailed as "an instructive essay in applied economics," crammed with "authoritative detail" culled from "the systematically kept accounts of a liberally managed estate," and telling a story "the broad outlines of which many great English landlords will recognise as analogous to facts which have come within their own experience."<sup>6</sup> One enthusiastic reviewer judged the book "a substantial contribution to the solution of the much-vexed land question."<sup>7</sup>

Does it follow, then, from this survey of Bedford's wealth that the duke's discontents—as George Russell would have them—were trivial or even bogus? Before concluding this it is worth noting that Bedford was more than a very rich man: he was a special sort of rich man—a landed magnate, a paternalist, a countryman. London was the place from which he drew most of his money, but it was not the place that mattered. The fall of his farming rents exacerbated a painful awareness of a threatened way of life. Compared with the new plutocrats whose erupting fortunes were less complicated by paternal responsibilities, public duties, and a commitment to English rural life, Bedford felt himself vulnerable, even deprived. Unlike the Cokes or Spencers, who had cleared their estates of a large debt by 1850, the Grahams had gone on carrying a large debt, mainly built up between 1815 and 1835, which amounted to £275,000 in 1861 and remained unchanged until the early twentieth century. Unaffected by the agricultural depression until 1894, Netherby's gross rental fell to £20,000 in 1905, a fall of 25 percent from a peak of £26,718 in 1882. Plans to sell part of the estate, first entertained in 1889, fell through for want of buyers. Then between 1910 and 1914 about half of the farmland was sold, the proceeds being applied to paying off 40 percent of the debt and investing the rest in the stock market. Some reduced the size of their estates in the upsurge of Edwardian land sales, but on the whole not dramatically.
