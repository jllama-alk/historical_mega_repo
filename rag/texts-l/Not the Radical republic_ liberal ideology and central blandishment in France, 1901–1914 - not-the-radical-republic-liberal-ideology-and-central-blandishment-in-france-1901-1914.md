# NOT THE RADICAL REPUBLIC: LIBERAL IDEOLOGY AND CENTRAL BLANDISHMENT IN FRANCE, 1901–1914<sup>\*</sup>

**DONALD G. WILEMAN**

ABSTRACT. *Madeleine Rebérioux was right to wonder whether France was truly a ‘Radical republic’ in the years between the Dreyfus affair and the Great War. Archives only opened or explored since Rebérioux published in 1975, and the re-interpretation of older newspaper sources, show that control of the Third Republic was still hotly contested in those years. The Radicals tried to build a republic in their own image, but in a situation where left and right were closely balanced, they were almost always foiled. Crucial to this process was a politically republican but socially conservative centre – best typified by the A.R.D. The A.R.D. wanted a Third Republic frankly favourable to the interests of big business. Since it held the parliamentary balance of power between the left and a right only partly republican, it generally got its way. Statistical sources also support this interpretation.*

Seventeen years ago, Madeleine Rebérioux took the received wisdom on France before the Great War and added a question mark to it – naming her account *La République Radicale?*<sup>1</sup> Historians have not rushed to answer the question, and it would certainly be difficult to find another label as alliterative or clear. Radicalism had an obvious shape and continuity.<sup>2</sup> Most of the groups around it did not. Treating the period from the Dreyfus affair to the Great War as the Radical era, and blaming the Radicals for everything that went wrong, also made a comfortably digestible chapter of it. Even Rebérioux – though reaching for a more fundamental revision – was generally content to attack the Radicals from their left, instead of from their right as most of her predecessors did.<sup>3</sup> It was difficult not to. Since 1975, new sources have broadened our perspective to the point where we can begin to answer Rebérioux. It now seems that few of the first fourteen years of the twentieth century really belonged to the Radical party. Instead – in a situation where left and right were closely balanced – the casting vote was almost always held by a politically republican but socially conservative centre – best typified by the Republican and Democratic Alliance of France (A.R.D. from its French initials). Almost always, the Alliance tilted the balance against the Radical republic.

This argument contributes to a trend of revision currently giving the Third Republic far more credit for political workability than had heretofore been done.<sup>4</sup> It tends to show two things: that both the Radicals and the centre (historically neglected groups, which often hated each other) contributed the most to a respectable level of stability, and that the Radicals and the Third Republic have too often been seen from the perspectives of their enemies, and of Anglo-Saxons quick to imagine that a democracy which does not work as theirs does, does not work at all.<sup>5</sup>

# I

What was French Radicalism? Far too often it is still assumed to be a version of *laissez-faire*, based on the assumption that individual freedom is the most important value in a democracy, and that individual freedom is best protected by keeping governments from doing anything. In France this view was popularized through the works of Emile Chartier – who wrote under the pseudonym ‘Alain’, and who named his most famous book *Éléments d’une doctrine radicale*.

More than twenty-six years ago Peter Larmour – whose sympathies lay elsewhere – was nevertheless forced to conclude that the Radicals in fact paid Alain almost no attention at all. Radical ideology certainly had its swirls and variations – like that of any consensus party. Its ranks contained a considerable number of small ‘o’ opportunists as well. Even so, Susan Mach has shown that an official core to the Radical party’s beliefs did exist – one quite different from *laissez-faire*. A general reading of the Radical press shows those beliefs to have been fairly consistent at any time from the beginnings of the twentieth century until the fall of France in 1940. Those beliefs were based on the small ‘l’ liberal doctrine of *solidarisme* – which urged using the power of the state to protect the individual’s freedom of choice. Classic radicalism was being

# II

Who were the Radicals? Were there enough of them in the national assembly to make a republic after their own hearts? Probably not. Certainly not every middling politician in France was a Radical. Elizabeth Wiskemann, for example, identified Paul Reynaud as a Radical. In fact the man hated everything the Radicals stood for. He devoted much of his political life to trying to build an effective mass organization of the centre and right which would fight Radicals, socialists and communists as members of the same awful tribe — efforts which the Radicals denounced as 'electoral fascism'.<sup>7</sup>

Which politicians labelled themselves as Radicals at election time? One should consider as most authentic those Radicals who not only used the label, but also joined the party and were at least sporadically willing to put their parliamentary votes behind the platforms drawn up by annual congresses of the Radical rank and file.

Other factors have also helped the Radical party's size and strength to be substantially over-estimated. Obvious among these is the only semi-vertebrate nature of the French party system in the early twentieth century. Every part of the Third Republic's politics was at least five shades less organized than its British and German equivalents. For decades after the consolidation of the republic in 1875, it was perfectly possible for a well-to-do notable (a corporation lawyer, a retired admiral, the biggest local estate owner, etc.) to win office as an independent non-party candidate. Generally, he would choose a common label with some historic resonance — like 'Republican'. Most such labels committed a candidate to nothing at all. They simply showed which broad slice of voting opinion he wished to appeal to. Those who arrived in the chamber of deputies often and easily jettisoned the flags under which they had run. They would join one of the even more numerous factions in the chamber. Usually the chamber faction which a deputy joined bore a family resemblance to his advertised position during the elections — but not always.

The slightly right-of-centre politicians who dominated the Third Republic's governments were precisely the most vaporous. Most of the sorts of *modérés* one found in the A.R.D. insisted that they were not really in 'politics' at all — and they went on insisting on it long after the Radicals (1901), the socialists (1904) and even some groups further right (such as the Republican Federation in 1903) started moving towards more modern, disciplined and coherent forms of party. The *modérés* could have formed a party based on their love of unrestricted capitalism and the social status quo. Occasionally, the A.R.D. thought about becoming that party. It repeatedly ran aground on some hard facts. The *modérés'* social position, their fortunes, educations and other gifts — all gave them power enough that they could safely indulge their personality

The Radicals’ difficulty is especially obvious in the results from 1910. The left was numerically stronger than the centre or right – but it was disunited. The Radicals were squeezed on their left by the Unified Socialist party (S.F.I.O.). This paid at least lip-service to Marx and – obedient to the Amsterdam congress of the Socialist International (1904) – gave only the most conditional and limited support to bourgeois Radicals after August of that year. To the Radicals’ right, socially conservative republican centrists of the A.R.D. type were always willing to bid for the votes of the faint-hearted or comfortable. The centre had much to offer: less onerous party discipline, better chances at office, rallying cries like free enterprise and (after about 1906) patriotism. Not least, it had connections with large corporations such as *Compagnie des Omnibus*, the *Société Nouvelle du Gaz de Paris* and the *Suez Canal company* – all of which had directors among A.R.D. members in the chamber of deputies. A.R.D. stalwarts tended to be offered briefs and directorships in the corporate world – whatever their origins had been. Centrism was still open to talent. Combined, these factors meant the Radicals lost many close votes in the ‘Radical’ republic.

# III

In the beginning was the Dreyfus affair. The question of Dreyfus' guilt or innocence split the phalanx of over 300 Opportunist republicans which had dominated the chamber of deputies and almost all French governments for a decade after the failure of General Boulanger's plebiscitarian campaign in 1889.<sup>15</sup> The affair's rising tide of unrest brought to power René Waldeck-Rousseau. His Government of Republican Defence eked out those of his fellow Opportunists willing to follow him with a minority of Radicals, with the socialist Alexandre Millerand, and with General Gallifet — the suppressor of the Paris Commune — now serving as a warning to the right and to the army itself.

From its own point of view the Waldeck-Rousseau government was more about restoring calm for capitalism than about revising the Dreyfus verdict. The premier himself decided for revision only after his appointment and one more careful review of the evidence. Waldeck-Rousseau's government was unheroic, but it survived where others had failed. It broke the army's trumped-up case and brought Dreyfus home. It also gave influence and some real power to Radicals and socialists, far sooner than their own gradual growth in popularity and improvements in organization would have done.<sup>16</sup>

The Dreyfus coalition outlasted the Waldeck-Rousseau government because the breach among pro-business republicans did. As the elections of 1902 approached, anti-Dreyfus Opportunists (or *Progressistes*, as they now preferred to be called), made no secret of the fact that they did not intend to forgive and forget, whatever the interests of their social class might suggest.<sup>17</sup> Meanwhile there were those on the left urging the creation of a Radical republic.

Camille Pelletan was an advanced Radical and one of the more influential men in the party. He denounced the way the early campaign divided along ministerial/anti-ministerial lines. This was a ploy of the right. Instead — he wrote — the campaign should be fought between parties, and as a contest between republicans and reactionaries. Waldeck-Rousseau would probably resign right after the election, so why fight it on his personality or that of his ministers? Beyond Dreyfus, little was left between the Socialists, Radicals and *laissez-faire* conservatives like the A.R.D. but division. The S.F.I.O. would not support a Radical government or join in a coalition with it, and repeatedly sought to outbid it on the left. The A.R.D. was standing by to provide the spare parts for a rightward-shifting majority. As Premier Clemenceau found both striking workers and the more progressive members of his own party difficult to get along with, he used more and more of those spare parts. Two votes on labour issues illustrate the process.

The major strikes of these years often featured the sort of violence in which no one stays neutral. Strikers and strike-breakers, unions, management, the police and (not infrequently) the army: all accused each other. Instead of trying to unscramble these eggs (a process probably productive of nothing but sustained ill-will), Radical premiers tended to cope by wiping the slate clean with general amnesties. Sarrien was the premier who succeeded Rouvier in March 1906 – when the latter's government fell over the technicalities of the separation. Faced with demands that crimes involving death or arson be prosecuted, he answered 'No'. What mattered, said Sarrien, was re-establishing social peace. His amnesty, Sarrien insisted, did not condemn, it did not condone. It forgot. Sarrien carried his point by 446 votes to 135, with Barthou's voice in the minority, along with the most reactionary conservatives. Sarrien resigned in October 1906 for health reasons, and was succeeded by Clemenceau. Two years later Clemenceau – repeatedly stung in his pride – joined the A.R.D. in denouncing the practice of amnesties as useless, and in demanding that 'anti-militarists, anti-patriots and fired civil servants' remain unamnestied. This left mainly the real criminals to benefit. When this was pointed out, and the S.F.I.O.'s Marcel Sembat pushed the point to a vote, Clemenceau and the A.R.D.'s spare-parts majority beat him.<sup>47</sup>

After Clemenceau's fall, France's direction was not just away from the Radical republic, it was towards the sort of do-nothing night-watchman state favoured by the A.R.D. Premier Aristide Briand announced that his new government would be based on 'appeasement' of the quarrels which had divided France. This was coded language for a centrism favourable to business. At a first glance, Briand seems an unlikely candidate to lead such a government. Briand was a former anarcho-syndicalist. At this time he still called himself a republican socialist, and had many friends on the left. Yet his 1902 candidacy against a more radical opponent had benefited from the official pressure of the government of René Waldeck-Rousseau. As one of Rouvier's cabinet ministers, Briand had eroded away as much of the separation of church and state as he could without sending the Radicals into outright revolt.<sup>50</sup> Briand in fact was 'evolving' to the right. He now made a speciality of persuading progressive majorities to follow policies so moderate they were almost regressive — to follow in fact what the A.R.D. called its policy of '*appaisement*, tolerance, and conciliation'.

In these efforts Briand was far more subtle than the A.R.D. knew how to be. When Alliance men like Flandin and Etienne suggested changing the Associations Law to make life easier for the clergy, it only gave the Radicals something to shoot at. Here was a cause that might rally a majority of the left. Briand, by comparison, was the Artful Dodger. He would discuss clericalism whenever the chamber of deputies wished to. He would be endlessly reassuring about the legality and correctness of all that was done — without ever showing signs of an argument intelligible enough to fight. Faced with a specific question, Briand would make jokes and then argue with the jokes. If interrupted in the middle of this process, Briand would pretend to be wounded — wondering why people asked him questions if they did not wish to hear his answers.<sup>51</sup>

# IV

When the cautiously progressive (and short-lived) Monis government fell in 1911, *Le Temps* dissented from the conventional wisdom. Monis' defeat, it insisted, had nothing to do with the PR bill which was its occasion. It had everything to do with Monis' failure to put enough socially conservative politicians in his cabinet.<sup>60</sup> *Le Temps* summed up: 'There are two majorities. That of the Left — and the real one.'<sup>61</sup>

Examination of the election results, of the people appointed to office, of divisions on important issues between the Dreyfus affair and the Great War, only seems to confirm *Le Temps'* view. The more leftward of those two majorities centred on — but was not limited to — the Radicals. The left majority gradually did better and better at elections, but was in office for only a couple of years under Combes and for a few months here and there thereafter — such as the tiny interval between the elections of 1914 and the outbreak of the First World War. Réberioux' question-mark looks amply justified. There was no Radical republic. It might be better to name this period 'the France of the Two Majorities'. Its quarrelsome, rotating elites we could call 'the Republicans of the Muddle'.
