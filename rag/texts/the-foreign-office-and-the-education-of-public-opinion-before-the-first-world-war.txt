On 30  November 1912  Sir  Arthur Nicolson, permanent under-secretary  at the
Foreign Office, wrote  a  letter concerning Austro-Russian rivalry  in the  Balkans
which had it been undated might well have been assigned to the month of July 1914:
If Austria attempts any coercive measures of any kind against Serbia  I am quite convinced...
that Russia would immediately take action  herself. The question then arises as to what part
Germany would play  in the event of Austria and Russia coming to blows... and ... I myself
have little doubt that Germany would stand by the side of Austria.  I am informed by military
experts that the Austrian Army could not really cope successfully with the Russian, and  it is
perfectly evident that Germany would not stand idly by and see her Ally defeated.
If Germany then is compelled to side with Austria, France, by her Treaty, will be compelled
to cooperate with Russia. Then comes another problem, and that is the attitude we ourselves
should pursue. 1
Eight days after this was written,  at a  conference in  Berlin with Admirals von
Tirpitz, von Muller, von Heeringen and General von Moltke, the Kaiser envisaged
the same sequence of events: Austria attacking Serbia, Russia intervening by moving
into Galicia, and Germany finding herself at war with Russia, France and England.
The most concrete of the decisions taken  at that conference was the adoption of a
suggestion made  by the  Kaiser, namely that  the  press be  used 'to  prepare  the
popularity of a war against Russia'. Immediately after the meeting, Muller wrote
to Chancellor Bethmann-Hollweg, telling him that  it had been agreed
to enlighten the people through the press of the great national interests, which would be at stake
also for Germany,  if a war were to break out over the Austro-Serbian conflict... The people
must not be in  a position of asking themselves only  at the outbreak of a great European war,
what are the interests that Germany would  be  fighting for. The people ought rather  to be
accustomed to  the idea of such  a war beforehand. 2
According  to  Professor Fischer,  the  indications  are  that such  a  campaign  of
'enlightenment' began without delay  in  Germany. The editor since 1906  of the
liberal Berliner Tageblatt  met Bethmann-Hollweg for the first time, by invitation,  in
January 1913, and was greatly struck by his insistence on the Russian menace and
the consequence  of the Balkan Wars. 3 'Attack from  the  Slavs' was the  'effective
slogan' chosen  for the campaign. 4

If any appeals were made by the Foreign Office for efforts to remedy the state
of affairs of which they complained, and to educate public opinion on to what they
considered to be right lines, these have not survived. It is true that, at the turn of
1911 Sanderson, believing that Grey's foreign policy would be better received if more
information were provided, suggested that more Blue Books be produced, and that
Hardinge, with whom he corresponded over this, agreed that it certainly ought to
be done.14 But Sanderson had retired as permanent under-secretary nearly six years
previously, and Hardinge was at the time viceroy of India. It is also true that Mallet
encouraged the publication of a letter by Balfour on the international situation; and
that some diplomats, such as Spring-Rice, were able to supply information to certain
journals and to comment upon draft articles submitted to them. 16 Such sporadic
efforts were token in the extreme. Sanderson's concern for Blue Books and Hardinge's
explanation of Grey's unpopularity in terms of his not spending enough time
discussing current affairs ' with politicians of both Houses' indicate the narrowness
of their conception of'public opinion'. A. J. Balfour's letter was directed at the
German public, and Mallet was not convinced that it would do much good:  ' I do
not think that a few isolated attempts of this kind will have much effect, as the
German Government have been purposely misleading the [German] public for
many years past.' In Mallet's opinion, it was ' even more important to educate public
opinion here,  in order that the Government of the day may have the necessary
backing when the next crisis occurs'.16 Yet it was precisely this which neither Mallet
himself nor, so far as can be seen, anyone else within the Foreign Office consistently
called for; it was precisely this which there was no sustained bid to undertake.

unrepresentative; its existence also accounts for why the Foreign Office felt no
overriding need to educate public opinion, why the ignorance of the latter was not
entirely unacceptable, and why the lack of means with which to consult public
